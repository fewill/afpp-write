SOURCE MATERIAL SUMMARY

SECTION A: Interaction Between Private Sector Rules and Banking Regulations
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 9606–9612
  Section heading: Chapter 5 – Governance Framework

  Key passage (lines 9606–9612):
  "Private sector rules and banking regulations in the payments industry mutually
  reinforce each other. On the one hand, banking regulations frequently establish
  legal frameworks that guide the private sector; on the other hand, private sector
  payment rules have been developed to enable and support the changes and
  development of new payment products. Thus, private sector rules and banking
  regulations complement each other, providing valuable support across various
  domains such as compliance, risk management, and consumer protection while
  fostering competition and innovation."

  Also: FFIEC booklet (lines 1941–1947):
  "A payment transaction may be covered by check law, Regulation E, association or
  clearing house rules, or private agreement, depending on what form the payment
  takes. Financial institutions should understand the laws and rules that apply to
  payments they handle and understand the associated legal risks and liabilities
  they take on with respect to those payments."

SECTION B: Payment Rails and Applicable Laws — Hierarchy by Rail
  Source: AFPP Handbook, Table 60, lines 9692–9721
  Section heading: "Payment rails governed by laws and regulations applicable to
  Faster Payments"

  Rail-by-rail hierarchy (Table 60):
  - FedNow:      Regulation E → Regulation J (Subparts B and C) → UCC Article 4A
                 → FedNow Operating Rules
  - RTP Network: Regulation E → UCC Article 4A → Service Level Agreements →
                 RTP Operating Rules → RTP Participation Rules
  - Same Day ACH: Regulation E → UCC Article 4A → EFTA → Nacha Operating Rules
  - Mastercard:  Regulation E → PCI DSS → Mastercard Operating Rules
  - Visa Direct: Regulation E → PCI DSS → Visa Operating Rules

  FedNow-specific hierarchy (FedNow Operating Procedures, lines 333–334):
  "The Board of Governors' Regulation J and the Federal Reserve Banks' Operating
  Circular 8 supersede these Operating Procedures to the extent of any
  inconsistency."

  UCC Article 4A and Regulation J (AFPP Handbook, lines 9664–9666):
  The Federal Reserve incorporated UCC Article 4A rules into Regulation J
  Subparts B and C for FedNow transactions.

SECTION C: Governance — Party Responsible
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 9723–9732
  Section heading: "Governance of laws and regulations applicable to Faster Payments"

  Key passage (lines 9723–9732):
  "The laws and rules governing Faster Payments are managed by various government
  agencies to ensure they're efficient, secure, and fair. Regulatory bodies at the
  federal level, including the Federal Reserve, the CFPB, and other agencies
  oversee and enforce regulations that govern Faster Payment solutions."

  Federal Reserve governance authority (FedNow Operating Procedures, lines
  330–334):
  "The Federal Reserve Banks reserve the right to terminate, restrict access,
  impose conditions or adopt controls associated with a Participant's access to
  the FedNow Service... Nothing in these Operating Procedures limits the Federal
  Reserve Bank's discretion to take actions beyond those outlined in these
  procedures."

SECTION D: Consumer vs. Non-Consumer Regulations
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 9733–9755
  Section heading: "Comparison between consumer and non-consumer regulations"

  Consumer regulations (lines 9734–9749):
  "Consumer regulations are laws that focus on and provide consumers with
  protection from fraud or unauthorized electronic payments. Consumers are defined
  as those who hold bank accounts for 'personal, family, or household' purposes
  and are differentiated from business customers. The Electronic Fund Transfer Act
  and Regulation E issued by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau are some of
  the main federal laws that protect consumers."

  Non-consumer regulations (lines 9750–9755):
  "In contrast, non-consumer regulations govern electronic transfers between
  businesses. These may include various operating rules established by the payment
  networks, interbank network rules as well as broader financial regulations such
  as the Bank Secrecy Act and Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regulations."

  Also: FFIEC booklet (lines 521–525):
  "ACH transactions to or from consumer accounts also are subject to the provisions
  of the Federal Reserve Board's Regulation E, Electronic Fund Transfers."

  And (lines 583–594):
  Regulation E gives consumer 60 days after statement to report unauthorized ACH
  debit; UCC does not contain specific re-credit procedures for checks.

SECTION E: Compare and Contrast Private Rule Sets and Federal Regulation
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 9756–9777

  Key distinctions (lines 9756–9777):
  - Federal regulations: enacted through structured legislative/regulatory process
    with public comment; may struggle to keep pace with technological change.
  - Private rule sets: immediate access to industry experts; foster innovation and
    competition; can adapt quickly to market evolution.
  - Federal statutes do not always cover payment liabilities between FIs; private
    sector has developed detailed loss-allocation systems for fraud.
  - The two systems complement each other: federal regulations set the foundation;
    private rules address operational detail and innovation.

  Also: FFIEC booklet (lines 1877–1910):
  Hierarchy includes: federal statutory law → federal regulations → network bylaws
  and agreements → contractual arrangements.

SECTION F: Objectives of Data Privacy Laws
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 9778–9788

  Key passage (lines 9778–9788):
  "Data privacy laws are significant for US Faster Payments systems to protect
  consumers from fraud, scams, and identity theft. The immediacy of funds
  availability in fast payment systems makes them attractive targets for fraudsters
  who could exploit the system before fraudulent activities are detected. Data
  privacy laws play a crucial role in safeguarding sensitive information and
  preventing breaches of privacy and data security that could lead to identity
  theft, financial harm, and fraud. These laws establish rules on data security and
  privacy, requiring institutions to obtain consumer consent before sharing data,
  anonymize data, and set minimum standards on data storage and transmission."

  Also: FFIEC booklet (lines 1892–1895):
  "Customer retail payment transaction records and corresponding account information
  are subject to the GLBA 501(b) provisions, and financial institutions must
  establish effective safeguards for protecting their customer information."

SECTION G: Application of BSA and AML to Faster Payments
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 9845–9870
  Section heading: "Application of the Bank Secrecy Act and Anti-Money Laundering
  regulations to Faster Payments"

  BSA purpose and core requirements (lines 9845–9852):
  "The Bank Secrecy Act of 1970 was implemented to prevent criminals from using
  financial institutions to hide or launder money... The BSA specifically imposes
  an AML program, record keeping, and reporting requirements on all financial
  institutions designed to prevent and detect money laundering and other crimes.
  It further specifies what an AML compliance program must include such as internal
  controls independent testing of responsible persons and training."

  Monitoring requirements specific to faster payments (lines 9858–9868):
  "These monitoring programs must include measures to detect payments that are
  suspicious in their value (i.e., frequent payments that are just under reporting
  thresholds or transaction limits), and their frequency. Monitoring programs must
  also account for other factors, such as the age of the account making these
  transactions. For example, accounts that have been opened recently, making
  payments just under reporting or transaction limit thresholds would be
  suspicious."

  Also: FFIEC booklet (lines 1947–1956):
  "The BSA requires financial institutions to have BSA/Anti-money laundering (AML)
  compliance programs and appropriate policies, procedures, and processes in place
  to monitor, identify unusual activity, and report suspicious activity. As such,
  all retail payment systems should be reviewed in terms of BSA/AML compliance
  requirements."

  ODFI OFAC responsibility (FFIEC booklet, lines 1961–1984):
  "For domestic ACH transactions, the ODFI is responsible for verifying that the
  originator of the ACH instruction is not a blocked party."

Skill Rating: JSQ

  Which federal law establishes the basic framework of rights, liabilities, and
  responsibilities of participants in consumer electronic fund transfer systems?

Responses:
  A. The Electronic Fund Transfer Act
  B. The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act
  C. UCC Article 4A

relationship between federal regulatory frameworks and private sector rules
requires synthesis across governance concepts that a 2-year practitioner

  How do private sector payment rules and federal banking regulations function
  in relation to each other within the faster payments governance framework?

  A. Federal regulations supersede private sector rules in all cases, with

     payment activities
  C. Federal regulations establish legal frameworks that guide the private sector,
     while private sector rules develop operational practices that support payment
     innovation within those frameworks

  C. The transfer amount exceeds the transaction limit established by the payment
     network's operating rules

Q8 — Conditional / SQ
Sub-competency: Explain the hierarchy of rules and regulations by payment rail
Skill Rating: SQ
Cognitive Level: Analysis
Rationale: Knowing the specific precedence order within FedNow governance
(Regulation J → Operating Circular 8 → Operating Procedures) and applying
it to a conflict scenario requires regulatory depth characteristic of a
5-year practitioner advising on governance risk.

Stem:
  When a FedNow participant's obligations under the FedNow Service Operating
  Procedures conflict with the requirements of Regulation J or Federal Reserve
  Operating Circular 8, which authority governs?

Responses:
  A. The FedNow Service Operating Procedures govern because they represent the
     most current and operationally specific guidance issued by the Federal
     Reserve Banks
  B. Regulation J and Operating Circular 8 govern because they supersede the
     FedNow Service Operating Procedures to the extent of any inconsistency
  C. The participant's internal compliance program governs because it must
     reconcile all applicable federal and network-level requirements

Answer: B

Source citation:
  Federal Reserve Banks. FedNow Service Operating Procedures (June 24, 2025),
  lines 333–334. Also: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 5 – Governance
  Framework, Table 60, lines 9692–9721.

Q9 — Conditional / SQ
Sub-competency: Explain the application of the Bank Secrecy Act and AML
regulations to faster payments
Skill Rating: SQ
Cognitive Level: Analysis
Rationale: Identifying structuring-style behavior — frequent payments just under
reporting thresholds in recently opened accounts — as the suspicious pattern
specific to faster payments requires synthesis of BSA/AML principles with
faster-payment-specific risk factors, characteristic of a 5-year practitioner.

Stem:
  Under what condition should a financial institution's faster payments monitoring
  program flag account activity as suspicious for potential money laundering?

Responses:
  A. The account holder initiates a Request for Payment message without a prior
     business relationship with the payment receiver
  B. The account's transaction volume exceeds the maximum transaction limit
     established by the faster payment network's operating rules
  C. A recently opened account makes frequent payments in amounts just under
     reporting thresholds or transaction limits

Answer: C

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 5 – Governance Framework, "Application
  of the Bank Secrecy Act and Anti-Money Laundering regulations to Faster
  Payments," lines 9858–9868.
