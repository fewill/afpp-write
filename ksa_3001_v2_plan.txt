SECTION A: Operational Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7605–7619

  - Definition: "operational risk represents the amount of loss related to
    unintentional errors, which may result in delayed, duplicated, or
    otherwise erroneous payments." (line 7605–7606)
  - Broader definition: risk arising from deficiencies in information
    systems, internal processes, human error, management failures, or
    external events that result in reduction, deterioration, or breakdown
    of services. (lines 7606–7608)
  - Unique FP challenge: 24/7/365 continuous availability requirement makes
    operational incidents uniquely severe. (line 7609)
  - Speed magnification: "Speed magnifies the impact of any operational
    incidents leading to delays or interruptions in Faster Payment services,
    and these interruptions are immediately noticeable to end users."
    (lines 7610–7611)
  - "A Faster Payment system being unavailable for a few minutes can cause
    many payments to fail; the consequences of operational downtime can
    therefore have even more serious implications than with traditional
    systems." (lines 7615–7617)
  - "Faster payment systems are subject to fraud and security risks, forming
    a large subset of operational risks." (line 7619)

  Source: FFIEC IT Examination Handbook: Retail Payment Systems Booklet,
          lines 1986–1987:
  - "Operational risk is the risk of loss resulting from inadequate or
    failed internal processes, people and systems, or external events."

SECTION B: Cross-Channel Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7622–7635

  - Definition: "Cross-channel risk occurs when the movement of fraudulent
    or illegal payment transactions from one payments channel to another is
    met with inconsistent risk management practices and lack of information
    sharing across payment channels about fraud." (lines 7622–7624)
  - Increased exposure: an FI offering faster payment products alongside
    traditional payment products (checks, wire transfers) faces increased
    cross-channel risk due to multiple channels available. (lines 7632–7633)
  - Speed enablement: "The speed and finality of faster payments reduce the
    time available for fraud detection and prevention. This can make it
    easier for fraudsters to potentially move funds quickly through different
    channels before an institution can review and detect suspicious
    activity." (lines 7633–7635)

  Source: FPC Glossary, lines 235–236:
  - "Occurs when the movement of fraudulent or illegal payment transactions
    from one payments channel to another (e.g., check payments to ACH) is
    met with inconsistent risk management practices and lack of information
    sharing across payment channels about fraud."

SECTION C: Compliance Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7637–7648

  - Primary driver: "The major factor contributing to this increase in
    compliance risks arises from the increased speed of most types of Faster
    Payments." (lines 7638–7639)
  - Concerns: insufficient time for AML, KYC due diligence, fraud
    prevention; automation creates its own risks. (lines 7639–7641)
  - Window problem: "The high velocity of Faster Payments provides a smaller
    window of opportunity for stakeholders to conduct thorough due diligence
    and real-time monitoring, potentially increasing the risk of non-compliance
    and fraud." (lines 7641–7643)
  - Balance: FIs must navigate between account holder expectations and
    regulatory requirements around financial crime controls. (lines 7644–7646)
  - Penalties: "Penalties for non-compliance can include fines, suspension,
    and even exit of the FI from the Faster Payment systems." (lines 7647–7648)

  Source: FFIEC Booklet, lines 1961–1984:
  - For domestic ACH, the ODFI is responsible for verifying the originator is
    not an OFAC blocked party; the RDFI is responsible for verifying the
    receiver is not a blocked party. (lines 1961–1969, 1975–1978)

  Source: OFAC Compliance Communiqué (instant_payment_systems_compliance_
          guidance_brochure.pdf), page 2:
  - "Domestic instant payment systems — i.e., those in which all transactions
    involve only accounts maintained at U.S. banks, excluding foreign
    correspondent accounts — generally pose a lower risk of sanctions exposure
    than instant payment systems that permit cross-border transactions."

SECTION D: Fraud Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7650–7686

  - Definition: "Fraud risk is the risk that a payment transaction will be
    initiated or altered in an attempt to misdirect or misappropriate funds."
    (lines 7650–7651)
  - Sources: dissatisfied/dishonest employees, third-party processors,
    originating company personnel, or outside parties (cyber thieves,
    fraudsters). (lines 7651–7653)
  - Three common fraud scenarios: (lines 7654–7668)
    1. End-user manipulation resulting in a payment instruction sent to
       the fraudster by a payer acting in good faith — credit-push fraud.
    2. Fraudster obtains payer/payee's sensitive payment data and initiates
       an unauthorized payment.
    3. Modification of a payment attribute (account number, amount, name)
       of a genuine instruction intercepted in transit.
  - Speed amplification: "the end-to-end speed of Faster Payments, especially
    the immediate availability of funds, makes Faster Payments more lucrative
    and susceptible to fraud risk than traditional payments." (lines 7672–7673)
  - FraudClassifier model — three classification questions: (lines 7674–7686)
    (1) Whether an authorized or unauthorized party initiated the payment
    (2) How the fraud was executed
    (3) What tactic was used to commit fraud

  Source: FPC Glossary, lines 43–44 (APP Fraud):
  - "When fraudsters deceive consumers or individuals at a business to send
    them a payment under false pretenses to an FI account controlled by the
    fraudster. Typically involves social engineering attacks and scams."

SECTION E: Liquidity Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7697–7711

  - Definition: "Liquidity risk is the current and potential risk to earnings
    or capital arising from a financial institution's inability to meet its
    obligations when they come due." (lines 7697–7698)
  - Settlement model dependency (lines 7700–7711):
    • FedNow and RTP: real-time settlement, pre-funded — liquidity risk is
      MINIMAL; each transaction settled in real time. (lines 7701–7703)
    • Mastercard Send and Visa Direct: prefunded, same-day/next-day
      settlement — liquidity risk is MITIGATED as funds are blocked at time
      of processing. (lines 7703–7705)
    • Same Day ACH: deferred net settlement — LIQUIDITY RISK BUILDS UP
      between settlement cycles. (lines 7705–7706)
  - Mitigation tools: prefunding, liquidity pools, collateral agreements,
    liquidity providers. (lines 7708–7710)

SECTION F: Technology Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7713–7728

  - Definition: "Technology risk refers to any potential for technology
    failures to disrupt business." (line 7713)
  - Six specific types of technology risk:
    1. External attacks (cyber-attacks) — loss/theft of sensitive data,
       damage to operating technologies, integrity of the system.
    2. Programming mistakes — operational errors and unplanned downtime
       during system updates.
    3. Human error — in building and maintaining IT systems.
    4. Electricity/power outages — technology systems vulnerable.
    5. Processing overload — theoretical possibility system reaches
       processing limits, unable to fulfill speed requirements.
    6. Outsourced infrastructure risk — system owners who outsource
       infrastructure operations face all above risks even if not directly
       connected to the system owner. (lines 7727–7728)

  Source: FPC Glossary, lines 823–824:
  - "Any potential for technology failures to disrupt a business or
    individual such as information security incidents or service outages."

SECTION G: Credit Risk
  Source: AFPP Handbook, lines 7730–7769

  - Definition: "Credit risk is the risk that a party to a transaction will
    not be able to provide the necessary funds, as contracted, for settlement
    to take place on the scheduled date." (lines 7730–7731)
  - ODFIs carry the greatest credit risk; losses generally arise from
    originator failure or bankruptcy. (lines 7740–7741)
  - Same Day ACH ODFI credit exposure range:
    "The ODFI's credit risk can range from 2 banking days (items that cannot
    be posted) to 60 calendar days (disputed ACH debit entries)." (lines
    7755–7756)
  - ACH debits compound credit risk: "Since ACH debits are generally sent on
    a recurring basis, the credit risk associated with them can compound
    making ACH debits a higher risk than credit entries." (lines 7757–7758)
  - RTP and FedNow — NO credit risk for RECEIVING a credit payment:
    "There is no credit risk associated with receiving an RTP or FedNow
    credit payment as both networks require funds be made available to the
    account holder by the receiving institution upon receipt of a Message
    Status Report." (lines 7767–7769)
  - RTP/FedNow origination services credit risk: providers can manage using
    a "good funds model" — debit sender's account before transmitting payment
    into the network. (lines 7761–7765)

  Source: FFIEC Booklet, lines 1821–1832:
  - ACH ODFIs: typical credit exposure of 3 days for credits; 60-day
    exposure for debits due to return potential.
  - RDFI's credit risk is minimal because it has the right to return items
    within NACHA guidelines.

Q1 — Factual / JSQ
Sub-competency: Describe the operational risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: JSQ
Cognitive Level: Recall
Rationale: Understanding that the 24/7/365 availability requirement makes
even brief interruptions immediately visible to end users — unlike
traditional payments — is foundational knowledge for a 2-year faster
payments practitioner.

Stem:
  Which characteristic of faster payment systems causes operational
  incidents to have a more immediate and severe impact than in traditional
  retail payment systems?

Responses:
  A. The requirement for continuous 24/7/365 availability means that even
     a brief service interruption causes payments to fail and is immediately
     noticeable to end users
  B. The requirement for participants to maintain prefunded settlement
     balances means any operational incident immediately reduces available
     liquidity across the entire network
  C. The requirement for mutual message signing and encryption means that
     any technology failure must be escalated to the network operator before
     transactions can resume

Answer: A

Source: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk
Management, "Operational risk" section, lines 7609–7618.

Q2 — Factual / JSQ
Sub-competency: Describe the fraud risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: JSQ
Cognitive Level: Recall
Rationale: Recognizing credit-push fraud — where a payer acts in good
faith after being manipulated by a fraudster — is working knowledge for
a 2-year faster payments practitioner handling fraud inquiries.

Stem:
  Which type of fraud occurs when a fraudster deceives a payer into
  voluntarily sending a payment to an account controlled by the fraudster?

Responses:
  A. Third-party fraud, in which a fraudster intercepts and modifies a
     genuine payment instruction after it has been submitted to the network
  B. Credit-push fraud, in which the payer acts in good faith after being
     manipulated into issuing a payment instruction bound for the fraudster
  C. Account takeover fraud, in which a fraudster gains unauthorized access
     to the payer's account credentials to initiate a payment without the
     payer's knowledge

Answer: B

Source: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk
Management, "Fraud risk" section, lines 7654–7655. Also: Glossary of Terms,
Faster Payments Council, "Authorized Push Payments Fraud (APP)" definition,
lines 43–44.

Q3 — Factual / SQ
Sub-competency: Describe the technology risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: SQ
Cognitive Level: Recall
Rationale: Knowing that outsourced infrastructure operations constitute a
distinct and named category of technology risk — one that exposes the
system owner even without a direct connection to the failure — requires
detailed knowledge of the Handbook's six-type technology risk taxonomy
beyond 2-year working knowledge.

Stem:
  Which of the following is identified as a distinct type of technology
  risk in faster payment systems?

Responses:
  A. The risk that participants fail to maintain minimum prefunded balance
     requirements during periods of peak transaction volume, causing
     payment rejections across the network
  B. The risk that encryption key compromise during mandatory key rotation
     exposes transaction data to unauthorized access by the network operator
  C. The risk that a technology failure affecting an outsourced
     infrastructure provider disrupts the faster payment system even when
     the system owner is not directly connected to the failure

Answer: C

Source: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk
Management, "Technology risk" section, lines 7713–7728.

Q5 — Procedural / JSQ
Sub-competency: Describe the liquidity risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: JSQ
Cognitive Level: Application
Rationale: Knowing that Same Day ACH is the faster payment rail most
exposed to liquidity risk because it uses deferred net settlement — unlike
FedNow and RTP which settle in real time — is working knowledge for a
2-year payments practitioner.

Stem:
  Which faster payment rail is most exposed to liquidity risk because it
  relies on deferred net settlement?

Responses:
  A. FedNow, because participants must manage Master Account balances at
     the Federal Reserve throughout each settlement cycle to avoid overnight
     overdrafts
  B. Same Day ACH, because liquidity risk builds up between settlement
     cycles rather than being eliminated through real-time settlement
  C. RTP, because Funding Participants must prefund a Prefunded Balance
     Account that may become insufficient during high-volume processing
     periods

Answer: B

Source: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk
Management, "Liquidity risk" section, lines 7700–7709.

Q7 — Conditional / JSQ
Sub-competency: Describe the cross-channel risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: JSQ
Cognitive Level: Analysis
Rationale: The condition that triggers increased cross-channel risk —
offering faster payments alongside traditional payment products with
inconsistent fraud detection practices — is a clear, single-rule condition
a 2-year practitioner should be able to apply.

Stem:
  Under what condition is a financial institution most exposed to
  cross-channel risk in its faster payments operations?

Responses:
  A. When the financial institution offers faster payment products alongside
     traditional payment products and applies inconsistent fraud detection
     practices across those payment channels
  B. When the financial institution processes payments for customers in
     multiple geographic regions and cannot synchronize fraud monitoring
     within the required faster payment response window
  C. When the financial institution uses a third-party service provider for
     faster payment processing while directly managing its traditional
     payment channels in-house

Answer: A

Source: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk
Management, "Cross-channel risk" section, lines 7622–7635.

Q8 — Conditional / SQ
Sub-competency: Describe the compliance risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: SQ
Cognitive Level: Analysis
Rationale: The OFAC guidance that domestic instant payment systems pose
lower sanctions risk than cross-border systems — specifically because all
accounts are maintained at U.S. financial institutions, excluding foreign
correspondent accounts — requires knowledge of the specific OFAC
Compliance Communiqué, a regulatory nuance beyond 2-year working knowledge.

Stem:
  According to OFAC guidance, under what condition does an instant payment
  system generally pose a lower risk of sanctions exposure?

Responses:
  A. When all transactions are subject to real-time automated OFAC
     screening by both the sending and receiving financial institutions
     before funds are made available to the account holder
  B. When all transactions involve only accounts maintained at U.S.
     financial institutions, excluding foreign correspondent accounts
  C. When the instant payment system restricts transaction values to
     amounts below the threshold at which OFAC reporting obligations
     are triggered for participating financial institutions

Answer: B

Source: OFAC Compliance Communiqué. Sanctions Compliance Guidance for
Instant Payment Systems (September 2022), "Domestic vs. Cross-Border
Payment Systems" section, page 2.

Q9 — Conditional / SQ
Sub-competency: Describe the credit risks present in faster payments
Skill Rating: SQ
Cognitive Level: Analysis
Rationale: The rule that a financial institution receiving a FedNow or RTP
credit payment faces NO credit risk — because both networks require funds
to be made available upon receipt of the Message Status Report — requires
synthesis of settlement model characteristics and credit risk concepts
across multiple payment rails, characteristic of a 5-year practitioner.

Stem:
  Under what condition does a financial institution that receives a FedNow
  or RTP credit payment face credit risk?

Responses:
  A. When the receiving financial institution posts funds to the account
     holder before receiving the final Message Status Report confirming
     settlement
  B. When the sending financial institution's intraday overdraft capacity
     is exhausted and the Federal Reserve temporarily suspends its ability
     to send value messages
  C. A receiving financial institution does NOT face credit risk upon
     receipt of a FedNow or RTP credit payment, because both networks
     require funds to be made available to the account holder upon receipt
     of the Message Status Report

Answer: C

Source: AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk
Management, "Credit risk" section, lines 7767–7769.
