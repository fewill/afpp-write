SECTION B: CIP Elements
  Lines: 8211–8257
  Section heading: "Customer Identification Program (CIP)"

  Legal authority:
  - USA PATRIOT Act requires FIs to have a written CIP. (lines 8214–8218)
  - BSA sets minimum requirements; CIP must be appropriate for institution's
    size and type. (line 8218)

  Three core CIP requirements (lines 8219–8231):
  1. Verify the identity of any person seeking to open an account.
  2. Maintain records of the information used to verify the person's identity.
  3. Consult government known or suspected terrorist lists to determine whether
     the person appears on any such list.

  Verification — information collected (lines 8235–8237):
  - Name, date of birth, address, and identification number
    (e.g., Social Security number for U.S. citizens)
  - Government-issued ID (passport, driver's license)
  - FIs may also verify without documents (contact, credit agencies,
    references from other FIs)

  Record retention (lines 8245–8247):
  - Records must be kept for five years after an account is closed.
  - Governed by 31 CFR 1010.430.

  Government list comparison (lines 8252–8257):
  - FIs must check new customers against OFAC SDN List and government
    terrorist lists within a reasonable time following account opening.
  - After account opening, FIs must periodically re-check account holders
    against OFAC's SDN List (separate ongoing requirement from initial CIP).

SECTION C: Responsibilities of Parties in a CIP
  Lines: 8265–8290
  Table 55: "Faster Payments Stakeholder Responsibilities in the Customer
  Identification Program"

  Financial institution (lines 8272–8281):
  - Set an appropriate CIP based on size and type of business
  - Obtain person's information upon account opening
  - Verify identity through risk-based procedures
  - File a suspicious activity report upon CIP failure
  - Comply with record-keeping requirements
  - Cross-check registered account against government lists
  - Provide adequate notice before verification procedures
  - When relying on another FI for a person's information, ensure that FI is
    regulated by a federal regulator and has adequate AML procedures
  - Ensure third-party providers adhere to CIP

  Third-party (lines 8284–8285):
  - Carry out services to FIs, regardless of data collection or management,
    according to the FI's CIP

  Customer (lines 8289–8290):
  - Share required information upon account opening
  - Update information upon changes

SECTION D: Additional Requirements for RfP Customers
  Lines: 8292–8306
  Section heading: "Additional requirements for Faster Payment customers that
  use Request-for-Payment functionality"

  Key passages:
  - Specific requirements apply to RTP users of the Request for Payment service.
    (line 8294)
  - TCH requires participants that send RfP messages to ensure the sender has a
    legitimate purpose to request funds. (lines 8294–8296)
  - Legitimate purpose — non-consumer sender: "The message is initiated by a
    non-consumer sender and is sent for a current sale or transaction, or the
    amount is due, owed, or otherwise agreed to be paid to the sender."
    (lines 8300–8301)
  - Legitimate purpose — consumer sender: "The message is initiated by a
    consumer sender to a receiver who is known to the sender and who is
    reasonably expected to receive the request from the sender."
    (lines 8305–8306)

----- FACTUAL QUESTIONS (3) -----

Q1 — Factual (Legal basis for CIP)
Sub-competency: List the elements of a CIP

Stem:
  Which federal law requires financial institutions to maintain a written
  customer identification program?

Responses:
  A. The USA PATRIOT Act
  B. The Electronic Funds Transfer Act
  C. The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act

Answer: A

Rationale: Lines 8214–8218 state: "Under the USA PATRIOT Act, financial
institutions are required to have a customer identification program (CIP)..."
The EFTA and Dodd-Frank govern different areas and do not establish the CIP
requirement.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  "Customer Identification Program (CIP)" section, lines 8214–8218.

---

Q2 — Factual (Record retention requirement)
Sub-competency: List the elements of a CIP

Stem:
  How long must a financial institution retain the records used to verify a
  customer's identity after the customer's account is closed?

Responses:
  A. Three years
  B. Five years
  C. Seven years

Answer: B

Rationale: Lines 8245–8247 state the five-year post-closure retention
requirement. Three years (EFTA standard) and seven years are plausible
period lengths that do not match the CIP record-keeping rule.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  "Record keeping and retention" subsection, lines 8245–8247. Regulatory
  basis: 31 CFR 1010.430.

---

Q3 — Factual (Customer responsibility in CIP)
Sub-competency: Describe the responsibilities of parties involved in a CIP

Stem:
  According to the 2026 AFPP Handbook, which party is responsible for sharing
  required identity information upon account opening as part of a customer
  identification program?

Responses:
  A. The third-party service provider
  B. The financial institution
  C. The customer

Answer: C

Rationale: Table 55 (lines 8289–8290) lists customer responsibilities as
sharing required information upon account opening and updating it upon changes.
The FI collects and verifies; the third-party performs services under the FI's
CIP; the customer provides the information.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  Table 55: "Faster Payments Stakeholder Responsibilities in the Customer
  Identification Program," lines 8289–8290.

----- PROCEDURAL QUESTIONS (3) -----

Q4 — Procedural (First step in identity verification)
Sub-competency: List the elements of a CIP

Stem:
  Which is the first step a financial institution should take when verifying
  a customer's identity as part of a customer identification program?

Responses:
  A. Collect the person's name, date of birth, address, and identification number
  B. Compare the person's information against the OFAC Specially Designated
     Nationals List
  C. File a suspicious activity report if the person's information cannot be
     confirmed

Answer: A

Rationale: Lines 8235–8237 state that "the first step in this process is to
collect the person's name, date of birth, address, and identification number."
Comparing against the OFAC SDN List and filing a SAR are subsequent steps
that follow initial collection and verification.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  "Verification" subsection, lines 8235–8237.

---

Q5 — Procedural (Ongoing OFAC list-checking after account opening)
Sub-competency: List the elements of a CIP

Stem:
  After a customer account is established, what should a financial institution
  do to fulfill its ongoing obligation related to government sanctions lists?

Responses:
  A. Collect updated identification documents from the account holder annually
  B. Periodically check account holders against the OFAC Specially Designated
     Nationals List
  C. File a suspicious activity report for each account that has not been
     updated within the prior year

Answer: B

Rationale: Lines 8255–8257 state that "once an account is established, FIs
must periodically check account holders against OFAC's SDN List, which is a
separate requirement from the CIP." Annual document collection and blanket
SAR filing are not the requirement.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  "Comparison with government lists" subsection, lines 8255–8257.

Q6 — Procedural (Third-party due diligence by FI)
Sub-competency: Describe the responsibilities of parties involved in a CIP

Stem:
  When a financial institution relies on another financial institution to
  collect customer identification program information, what should the
  relying institution verify?

Responses:
  A. That the other financial institution participates in the same faster
     payments network as the relying institution
  B. That the other financial institution is regulated by a federal regulator
     and has adequate anti-money laundering procedures
  C. That the other financial institution has filed its annual audit report
     with the applicable network operator

Answer: B

Rationale: Table 55 (lines 8279–8281) states that when relying on another FI,
the institution should "ensure that it is regulated by a federal regulator and
has adequate AML procedures." Network participation and audit filing are not
the conditions stated for this reliance.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  Table 55: "Faster Payments Stakeholder Responsibilities in the Customer
  Identification Program," lines 8279–8281.

Q7 — Conditional (When FI must file a SAR related to CIP)
Sub-competency: Describe the responsibilities of parties involved in a CIP

Stem:
  Under what condition is a financial institution required to file a suspicious
  activity report in connection with its customer identification program?

Responses:
  A. The customer identification program fails
  B. The customer initiates a faster payment transaction of more than $10,000
  C. The customer declines to update personal information after account opening

Answer: A

Rationale: Table 55 (lines 8273–8274) lists "File suspicious activity report
upon CIP failure" as a financial institution responsibility. Transaction
amounts and information update refusals are not the specified trigger.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  Table 55: "Faster Payments Stakeholder Responsibilities in the Customer
  Identification Program," line 8273.

Q8 — Conditional (Why faster payments heighten onboarding risk)
Sub-competency: Explain why onboarding programs are important for managing
risk related to faster payments

Stem:
  Why does the irrevocable nature of faster payment transactions make proper
  customer onboarding especially important for managing risk?

Responses:
  A. Irrevocable transactions reduce the number of available payment rails,
     requiring more rigorous network registration
  B. Irrevocable transactions are more easily abused by fraudsters, increasing
     the legal risks and costs associated with fraud cases
  C. Irrevocable transactions require financial institutions to verify customer
     identity more frequently than reversible payment types

Answer: B

Rationale: Lines 8198–8200 state: "As Faster Payment methods, especially those
that are irrevocable, are easier to abuse by fraudsters, the number of cases
can increase significantly and thus the legal risks and costs along with them."
The irrevocability is the condition that elevates fraud risk — not network
availability or verification frequency.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  "Importance of onboarding programs for managing risk related to Faster
  Payments" section, lines 8198–8200.

Q9 — Conditional (Legitimate purpose for non-consumer RfP sender)
Sub-competency: Identify additional requirements for faster payment customers
that use Request for Payment functionality

Stem:
  Under what condition does a non-consumer sender satisfy the legitimate
  purpose requirement for sending a Request for Payment message on the RTP
  network?

Responses:
  A. The message is sent for a current sale or transaction, or the amount is
     due, owed, or otherwise agreed to be paid to the sender
  B. The message is initiated to a receiver who is known to the sender and who
     is reasonably expected to receive the request
  C. The message is accompanied by a signed origination agreement between the
     sending and receiving financial institutions

Answer: A

Rationale: Lines 8300–8301 state the non-consumer sender condition: "The
message is initiated by a non-consumer sender and is sent for a current sale
or transaction, or the amount is due, owed, or otherwise agreed to be paid to
the sender." Response B describes the consumer sender condition (lines 8305–
8306). Response C is a plausible distractor but not stated in the source.

Source citation:
  AFPP Handbook (2026). Nacha. Chapter 3 – Faster Payments Risk Management,
  "Additional requirements for Faster Payment customers that use
  Request-for-Payment functionality," lines 8300–8301.
