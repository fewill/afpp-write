payments industry with a comprehensive knowledge of faster

§ 4A-303. ERRONEOUS EXECUTION OF PAYMENT ORDER.
(a) A receiving bank that (i) executes the payment order of the sender by
issuing a payment order in an amount greater than the amount of the
sender's order, or (ii) issues a payment order in execution of the sender's
order and then issues a duplicate order, is entitled to payment of the
amount of the sender's order under Section 4A-402(c) if that subsection is
otherwise satisfied. The bank is entitled to recover from the beneficiary of
the erroneous order the excess payment received to the extent allowed by
the law governing mistake and restitution.
(b) A receiving bank that executes the payment order of the sender by
issuing a payment order in an amount less than the amount of the sender's
order is entitled to payment of the amount of the sender's order under
Section 4A-402(c) if (i) that subsection is otherwise satisfied and (ii) the
bank corrects its mistake by issuing an additional payment order for the
benefit of the beneficiary of the sender's order. If the error is not corrected,
the issuer of the erroneous order is entitled to receive or retain payment
from the sender of the order it accepted only to the extent of the amount of
the erroneous order. This subsection does not apply if the receiving bank
executes the sender's payment order by issuing a payment order in an
amount less than the amount of the sender's order for the purpose of
obtaining payment of its charges for services and expenses pursuant to
instruction of the sender.

2006.4: Identify barriers and solutions to cross-border interoperability of faster payments rails
Barriers to cross-border payments
Screening procedure barriers
AML and sanctions screening represent obstacles to cross-border interoperability of
Faster Payments systems. This is because receiving institutions have to trust that
screening and onboarding procedures in other markets are accurate and meet the same
requirements as in the home market. Furthermore, the nature of Faster Payments means
that time to verify transactions is limited, meaning that automation is key as well as a
source of risk. A lack of universal AML and sanctions compliance requirements across
jurisdictions, varying regulatory approaches and onboarding requirements, and fears of
reputational/financial loss by FIs lead to significant risk in the processing of cross-border
payments.
Fragmentation in International Data Standards
International data standards present significant barriers to cross-border faster payments,
primarily arising from fragmentation and uncertainty within data frameworks. One
significant challenge lies in the inconsistency across jurisdictions regarding the
implementation of rules and standards. This fragmentation extends to jurisdiction-specific
requirements for messaging formats and legal documentation, resulting in manual
interventions and higher rates of payment rejections. Moreover, payment service providers
grapple with balancing various obligations under different data frameworks, such as
regulatory and supervisory requirements, third-party provider usage, and data security and
privacy mandates.

Foreign exchange barriers
Foreign exchange can present obstacles to cross-border instant payments due to currency
conversion costs, transparency concerns, tight SLAs, fluctuations in exchange rates, and
FX market operating hours, among other factors. These factors collectively increase the
cost, uncertainty, and risk associated with these transactions as well as potentially
harming the end user experience. Currency conversions can introduce hurdles to payment
fulfillment due to the additional transaction costs involved, fluctuating exchange rates, and
desire to conclude relationships with FX service providers.

1001.3: Describe the impact and benefits of faster payments for stakeholders
Impact and benefit of Faster Payments for stakeholders
Request-for-Payment
Request-for-payment is a type of non-payment message enabling
businesses or individuals to initiate a request that the recipient initiate an
instant credit transfer. Unlike with a direct debit, an RfP still gives the
recipient of the RfP (the sender of the payment) control as to a) whether
the transaction is accepted or not and b) the timing of said payment. The
FedNow and RTP networks provide RfP functionality, although the two are
not interoperable between the networks.
This functionality provides several benefits, including greater convenience
and flexibility for consumers in paying their bills – including the ability to
avoid late payments – or requesting funds from friends or family. For
billers, RfPs enable a sort of account validation when setting up a new
business relationship (if the account does not exist, the RfP will not work
and if the account does not belong to the intended payee, the RfP is
unlikely to be accepted). They also do not require mandates to be set up or
held onto because the recipient of the RfP has complete control as to if and
when the payment is made. Billers also benefit from straight-throughprocessing (STP) and enhanced reconciliation due to the RfP message
itself.

What benefit does an RfP provide to a payer that a
direct debit does NOT provide?
a. Automation of the payment process
b. Establishment of authorization mandates
c. Control over transaction acceptance*

3001.2: Describe the cross-channel risks present in faster payments
Types of risks present in Faster Payments
Operational risks present in Faster Payments
Operational risk is the risk of financial loss and undermined user confidence resulting from
the failure of internal processes and systems because of human error or external events.
Operational risk poses a huge challenge because of the need for continuous system
availability 24/7/365. This imposes significant demands on operational reliability for Faster
Payments and participants. Speed, on the other hand, magnifies the impact of any
operational incidents leading to delays or interruptions in Faster Payment services, and
these interruptions are immediately noticeable to end users. The repercussions of an
operational incident in Faster Payments may manifest much sooner in comparison to
traditional retail payments, where service interruptions or slowdowns could go unnoticed.
Cross-channel risks present in Faster Payments
Due to the speed of faster payments, processes related to payments within banks speed
up as well. Nevertheless, banks have multiple departments that are involved in handling
payment processing, so even though a bank has the technology to connect to a payment
rail and process faster payments for its customers, risks could arise from other
departments’ processes not being adjusted to keep up with that speed. Key departments
that could be impacted are compliance and risk management, operations, IT, treasury
services and customer service.
Liquidity risks present in Faster Payments
Faster payment systems and their participants are susceptible to liquidity risks arising from
the possibility of a counterparty, whether a participant or another entity, lacking adequate
funds to settle their financial obligations. Liquidity risks in Faster Payment systems depend
on the type of settlement model being used by the system. For systems such as FedNow
and RTP, which utilize real-time settlement, liquidity risk is minimum because settlement is
pre-funded and each transaction is settled in real time during payment processing. Same
Day ACH FI relies on deferred net settlement so there is liquidity risk that builds up
between the settlement cycles. Liquidity is primarily required at the end of each settlement
cycle, introducing potential risks if inter-PSP settlement occurs beyond normal business
hours.

Refining and Improving an Item

Pose a complete, unambiguous question.
●

Use a complete question format and avoid the incomplete sentence format. Most of the text you write should
appear in the stem (not the response options).
– Do NOT try to trick the test taker with complex or confusing clauses and sentence structures.

●

Avoid the use of negatively phrased questions unless absolutely necessary.

●

Fill-in-the-blank and True/False questions are NOT allowed.

– Use generic position titles (e.g., risk manager, board of directors, leadership); do NOT use “he” or “she”.

– Ensure they do not cover overlapping content.
– Vary the position of the correct response.

Avoid questions that focus on trivial topics or opinions.
●

Write questions that test relevant and important topics.
– WHO, WHAT, WHEN: Facts, useful definitions of terms, critical dates or time frames, monetary limits, significant
events, etc.

– HOW: Proper sequences of actions or behaviors, the next or first step in a process, the most effective technique to
use for a given objective/task, etc.
– WHY, SO WHAT: The value of different pieces of information relative to a cause/objective, the rationale for doing
something in a particular way, the legal basis for a policy or task, etc.

Nacha Style Guidelines

Nacha Style Guidelines
Word Usage
●

Use mitigate in relation to risk rather than “prevent”.​

●

Use financial institution instead of specific types of financial institutions (e.g., bank, credit union),
unless the type has a bearing on the correct response.​

●

Use should instead of “would,” “could,” “might,” or “may.”

Formatting
●

Use italics to emphasize FFIEC source references that are included in the stem. For example:​
– According to the FFIEC IT Examination Handbook: Retail Payment Systems Booklet…​

●

Write the word “not” in all capital letters (i.e., NOT).

●

Do NOT use underlining or boldface type.

●

NEW! Do NOT use the registered trademark or copyright symbols (e.g., RTP®, FedNow®)

Nacha Style Guidelines
Citations
●

Cite the source in the stem if the answer varies depending on which source or
perspective one takes.
– Example: “According to Operating Circular 4,…”

●

If using a citation to the FFIEC IT Examination Handbook, identify the specific booklet.
– Example: “According to the FFIEC IT Examination Handbook: Retail Payment Systems
Booklet…​”

●

When referencing the Nacha Operating Rules in an item, always write the full name.
– Do NOT use shortened versions such as ACH Rules or Nacha Rules.​
